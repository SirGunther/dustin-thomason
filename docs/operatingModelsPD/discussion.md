The priority I would lead with is that we should focus on productivity multipliers: the foundational process and technical work that reduces repeated friction across future efforts.

The reason this matters is that uncertainty is expensive when it moves downstream. When a ticket enters refinement without enough context, when a business decision comes forward without a recommended direction, or when a feature depends on backend foundations that do not exist yet, the team can still move forward, but the cost shows up later as rework, workarounds, blocked time, repeated discussion, and slower delivery.

In other words, missing foundations create repeated downstream cost.

That is why some work is worth prioritizing even if it is not the easiest immediate feature or fix. A foundational improvement may take more focused effort up front, but if it removes friction from many future requests, it becomes a productivity multiplier.

The larger principle is that we should design our operating model around connectedness and change. A local gap rarely stays local; it affects handoffs, timelines, architecture, customer experience, and team communication.
