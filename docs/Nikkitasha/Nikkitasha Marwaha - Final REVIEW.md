# Brutal Review — Nikkitasha Marwaha (Final.docx)

A "Problem Check" applied to the résumé as if it were an argument being worked.
The résumé is the discussion; the reader (a hiring manager) is the audience it's
trying to convince. Every claim below cites the document's own words.

## In brief

The résumé positions Nikkitasha as a combined cybersecurity / project-management /
technical-writing professional, leading with a federal risk-and-compliance (GRC)
narrative built on RMF/FISMA/FedRAMP work at TACG since 2019. The most specific,
repeatable evidence clusters in compliance and audit; the project-management and
technical-writing claims sit under the same employer and are lighter. As written,
the document asks the reader to accept three professional identities at once.

# What question is the résumé actually answering?

This is the line-level version of the framing check above: do the bullets, read in
their own context, actually answer the question the section claims to be answering?

### The question the résumé *says* it's answering
> "Can this person do cybersecurity, project management, *and* technical writing?"
— that's the header's promise.

### The question it's *actually* answering
> "Can this person run federal risk and compliance work and keep it audit-ready?"

The profile opens "cybersecurity, risk, and compliance professional," the Key
Qualifications are four of five GRC-flavored, and the deepest bullets (RMF/FISMA/
FedRAMP, POA&M, SAR QA, Archer, control-count reporting) are all compliance. The
GRC question is answered well. The other two are answered thinly:

- **Project Manager** rests on a single engagement — "SBOM ingestion and vulnerability
  scanning system" — described in generic PM verbs ("owning scope, schedule," "tracked
  tickets, issues, and action items"). It demonstrates *a* project, not a PM track record.
- **Technical Writer** is real but reads as compliance output ("monthly compliance
  dashboards," "SAR quality assurance," "POA&M submissions") — i.e., it's more evidence
  for the GRC answer than for a standalone writing role.

### The question it *should* be answering
> "What single role am I the strongest candidate for, and does every line move that case forward?"

Pick the lane the evidence already supports — **federal cybersecurity GRC / compliance**
— and let PM and writing become proof *of that*, not competing headlines. Concretely:

1. **Header:** collapse three titles to one primary (e.g., "Cybersecurity Risk & Compliance Specialist"), with PM/writing as a secondary line if needed — not three equal claims.
2. **Profile:** fix the "9+ years securing systems" claim so it matches the timeline (count security tenure honestly, or reword to "9+ years across federal IT and security programs").
3. **TACG block:** either date the three sub-roles or merge them into one role with grouped accomplishments, so it stops reading as three undated jobs.
4. **Every "100%" / "trusted advisor" line:** attach a number or an artifact, or cut it.
